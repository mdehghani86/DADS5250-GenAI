PART 02 Why Pipelines Matter
On Camera ~1.5 min

Until now, you have been making single API calls. One prompt in, one response out. But real AI applications are rarely that simple. You might need the model to remember what the user said three messages ago. You might need it to search a database before responding. You might need it to call a tool, format the output, and pass it to a second model.

LangChain gives you pre-built components for every part of an AI pipeline -- and a standard way to connect them together.

Video 2: "LangChain Foundations"

KEY MESSAGE Four building blocks: LLMs, Prompt Templates, Memory, Chains. LCEL connects them all.
PART A The Four Building Blocks
Voiceover + Animation ~4 min

LangChain is an open-source framework for building AI pipelines, created by Harrison Chase in late 2022. There are four core elements.

First, LLMs -- a unified interface to every major model. Same code, swap providers in one line. Second, Prompt Templates -- reusable prompts with variables. One template, a thousand inputs.

Third, Memory -- LLMs are stateless by default, so LangChain adds conversation history across calls. Fourth, Chains -- connecting steps together. The output of step one becomes the input of step two.

Storyboard -- Four Building Blocks
MEDIA TEAM: Each block should feel substantial and modular -- like real building components. The swap animation inside the LLM block (GPT to Gemini to Claude) demonstrates provider-agnostic design. The memory block should show messages persisting (not disappearing like in earlier modules). The final assembly is the payoff -- four independent pieces becoming one system.
PART B Workflow Modeling for AI
Voiceover + Animation ~90 sec

Here is what LangChain actually does, with an example you already know.

Imagine a process inside an Amazon fulfillment center. You click "buy" on your phone, and that one click kicks off a whole pipeline. A picker walks the aisle and grabs your item from the shelf. A packer drops it in the right-sized box. A labeler prints the shipping label and slaps it on. A sorter routes the box to the truck going to your zip code. And a driver delivers it to your door. Each station has one job. Each station is independent. And the conveyor belt between them is what makes the whole thing work -- it carries the package from one station to the next, without anyone having to carry it by hand.

LangChain does the exact same thing for AI workflows. Each of the four building blocks you just saw -- prompt templates, LLMs, memory, chains -- is a station on your AI fulfillment line. Input comes in one end. Each component does its one job. The finished output comes out the other end.

And here is the beautiful part. Just like Amazon can swap out the labeling machine without shutting down the entire warehouse, you can swap one component in your LangChain pipeline -- change the LLM, update the prompt template, add memory -- without touching the rest of your application. That is what makes LangChain powerful. LangChain is a fulfillment center for AI.

PART C LCEL -- The Pipe Operator
Voiceover + Code Display ~2 min

The modern way to build chains is called LCEL -- LangChain Expression Language. It uses the pipe operator to connect components. It is clean, it is readable, and it is powerful.

Here is a quick preview of what that looks like in code.

chain = prompt | llm | output_parser

result = chain.invoke({"topic": "supply chain optimization"})
Storyboard -- LCEL Pipe Flow
MEDIA TEAM: The pipe operator is the visual signature of LCEL. Make the | symbols feel special -- they are not just syntax, they are the connectors that make the whole thing work. The data flow below should mirror the code above perfectly, showing what each | actually does. Students should be able to trace data from input to output.

Video 4: "AI Meets the Beer Game"

Voiceover + animation + simulation -- 7-9 minutes -- 3 segments
KEY MESSAGE Four AI agents chained together reproduce the bullwhip effect -- supply chain theory meets LangChain.
PART A The Beer Game Explained
Voiceover + Animation ~3 min

Now let us take LangChain and apply it to something real -- the Beer Game.

The Beer Game is a supply chain simulation invented at MIT in the 1960s. It has been used in business schools around the world to teach one of the most important concepts in supply chain management: the bullwhip effect.

Here is the idea. You have four roles in a supply chain: Retailer, Wholesaler, Distributor, and Factory. Each one orders inventory from the next. The problem? There are delays. And when demand changes at the retail level, each player overreacts. Small changes in demand amplify into massive swings at the factory level.

Storyboard -- The Beer Game & the Bullwhip
MEDIA TEAM: This is a signature animation. The amplification from Retailer to Factory must be dramatic and intuitive. Start calm, then the demand spike ripples through the chain with increasing intensity. The wave visualization below the nodes makes the bullwhip effect unmistakable. Use this asset as the background for the simulation results in Segment C.
PART B AI Agents in the Supply Chain
Voiceover + Animation ~2 min

Now here is where it gets interesting. What if each supply chain role is an AI agent? Each agent has its own prompt, its own inventory data, its own decision-making logic. And we chain them together using LangChain.

The Retailer agent sees customer demand and places an order. That order flows to the Wholesaler agent. The Wholesaler sees the order and makes its own decision. And so on, up the chain.

Storyboard -- Each Role Becomes an AI Agent
PART C The Bullwhip Emerges
Voiceover + Data Visualization ~2 min

When you run this simulation over 20 rounds, you will see the bullwhip effect emerge naturally -- from the AI agents' decisions. This is not a toy example. This is how companies model supply chain decisions.

Storyboard -- 20-Round Simulation Result
MEDIA TEAM: The gradual divergence of the four lines is the entire lesson. Start them close together, then watch them spread apart. The Factory line should look almost chaotic by round 15-20 while the demand line stays flat. The stat card with "9.5x amplification" is the quantitative punchline. Consider adding faint sound effects -- a calm hum for the demand line, increasingly urgent pings for the Factory line.
FFT Food for Thought — Who Was Jay Forrester?
On Camera ~40 sec Medium close-up

Before we wrap this up, let me leave you with one small question.

You just played the Beer Game and watched the bullwhip effect emerge out of nothing. But have you ever stopped and asked yourself — who invented that game? And what kind of mind sits down and says, "I am going to build a simulation that proves supply chains amplify their own noise"?

I am not going to tell you. Take five minutes after this video and search two names: Jay Forrester and system dynamics. Read about the professor at MIT who invented this game in the 1960s, what he was working on before this (hint: it involved WWII radar and one of the earliest computers ever built), and how he created an entire field of study called system dynamics almost single-handedly. It is one of those stories that makes you realize the most influential people in a field are often the ones whose names you have never heard.

Because here is a habit I want you to build: when a tool shows up in your work over and over again, get curious about its history. The engineers and managers who understand why a simulation was invented always learn more from playing it than the ones who just play.

Go look it up. I will see you in the next video.
