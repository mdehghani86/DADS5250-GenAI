PART 1: OPENING HOOK
------------------------

I want to tell you something that changed how I think about technology.

Last semester, I received 47 emails in a single week from people I had never met — asking to collaborate, requesting meetings, pitching products. Every one of them was well-written. Professional tone. Personalized to my research.

I started reading them more carefully. And I realized — most of these were not written by the people who sent them. They were written by AI.

That was my wake-up call.

If this many people are already using AI to reach me, what does that mean for the students sitting in my classroom? Are they going to be the ones receiving AI-generated content for the rest of their careers? Or are they going to be the ones building the tools that create it?

That question is why this course exists.

[ANIMATION: Envelope icons flooding in, then flipping to show "AI-generated" labels]


PART 2: WHAT IS GENERATIVE AI?
------------------------

So what exactly is Generative AI?

In plain English — imagine you had a colleague who had read every book, every research paper, every website ever published. And whenever you had a question, you could ask them. Not search the web — actually think through the problem and generate a new, original answer.

That is what a large language model does.

It does not copy and paste from a database. It generates. It creates something new every time, based on patterns it learned from an enormous amount of human knowledge.

The breakthrough came from a 2017 Google paper called "Attention Is All You Need." That paper introduced the Transformer architecture — the engine behind GPT, Gemini, Claude, and every major AI system you use today.

And when OpenAI released ChatGPT in late 2022, the world noticed. One million users in five days. Instagram took two and a half months to reach that number. Netflix took three and a half years.

[ANIMATION: Growth chart — ChatGPT vs Instagram vs Netflix time to 1M users]

People were hungry for this. And the technology has only accelerated since.


PART 3: REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES
------------------------

Let me give you two examples of what is happening right now.

In drug discovery, a company called Insilico Medicine used AI to identify a drug candidate for a rare lung disease — in 18 months. The traditional timeline is four to five years. That is not a projection. That drug is in human trials today.

In software engineering, Google's CEO confirmed that over 25 percent of all new code at Google is now generated by AI. At Microsoft, GitHub Copilot writes code alongside millions of developers every day.

A life-saving drug in 18 months. A quarter of Google's codebase written by AI. This is not a future scenario — this is March 2026.

[ANIMATION: Two side-by-side panels — Drug timeline (5 years vs 18 months), Google code pie chart (25% AI)]


PART 4: WHY THIS MATTERS FOR YOUR CAREER
------------------------

Here is what I keep hearing from hiring managers across industries.

AI will not replace engineers. But engineers who know how to use AI will replace those who do not.

A colleague of mine in life sciences put it this way: "AI is going to be like Excel." In the early 2000s, spreadsheet literacy became a baseline expectation. AI literacy is heading in the same direction — faster.

The question is not whether you will use AI in your career. The question is whether you will know how to direct it, evaluate it, and build with it.

That is exactly what we are going to do.


PART 5: WHAT YOU WILL BUILD
------------------------

By the end of this course, you will have built working applications — not toy examples.

You will connect to AI models through their APIs. You will design prompts that produce reliable, structured output. You will build RAG pipelines grounded in your own documents. You will create autonomous agents that reason, plan, and take actions. And you will deploy real workflows using the latest tools — Claude Agent SDK, OpenAI Agents SDK, Google ADK, MCP, and more.

These are portfolio-ready skills. The kind that show up in job interviews and on your resume.

Let us get started.
