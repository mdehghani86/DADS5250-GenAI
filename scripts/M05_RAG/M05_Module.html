Imagine you are taking an open-book exam. You have your textbook, your notes, your slides. You do not need to memorize everything -- you just need to know where to look and how to use what you find.

That is exactly what RAG does for an AI model. It gives the model a reference library so it does not have to guess.

Without RAG, an AI model only knows what it learned during training -- which has a cutoff date and does not include your private data. Ask it about your company's Q4 results? It will make something up. Confidently. Convincingly. And completely wrong.

RAG fixes this. Before the model generates an answer, it retrieves relevant information from YOUR documents. Then it generates a response grounded in real data -- not guesses.

An AI that cannot access your data is just guessing. After this module, yours will not.

Let me show you the three steps behind every RAG system. Chunk. Embed. Retrieve. That is the entire pattern.

Step 1: Chunk. You take your documents -- PDFs, text files, web pages -- and you split them into smaller pieces. Why? Because an LLM has a limited context window. You cannot feed it your entire knowledge base at once. So you break it into manageable chunks -- paragraphs, sections, maybe 500 words each.

Step 2: Embed. Each chunk gets converted into a vector -- a list of numbers that captures the meaning of that text. This is the magic of embeddings. Words with similar meanings have similar numbers. "King" and "Queen" are close in embedding space. "King" and "Bicycle" are far apart.

Step 3: Retrieve. When a user asks a question, that question also gets embedded into a vector. Then the system finds the chunks whose vectors are most similar to the question. Those chunks -- and only those chunks -- get passed to the LLM as context.

The model does not search your documents. Embeddings do. The model only sees the relevant pieces.

Think of it this way. The embedding is the librarian. It knows where everything is. The LLM is the expert. It knows how to synthesize and explain. Together, they give you grounded, accurate answers.

Let me walk you through building a complete RAG pipeline from scratch. Five steps. That is the entire pattern. Everything else is optimization.

Step 1: Load documents. This could be PDFs, text files, web pages. LangChain has document loaders for all of them. One line of code to load a PDF.

Step 2: Split into chunks. We use RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter -- the Swiss army knife of text splitting. It tries to split on paragraphs first, then sentences, then words. You set a chunk size -- say 500 characters -- and an overlap -- say 50 characters -- so each chunk shares some context with its neighbors.

Step 3: Create embeddings. We use OpenAI's embedding model -- text-embedding-3-small. Each chunk becomes a vector of 1536 numbers. These numbers capture the semantic meaning of that text.

Step 4: Store in ChromaDB. ChromaDB is an open-source vector database. It stores your embeddings and lets you search by similarity. Think of it as a database optimized for "find me things similar to this" instead of "find me things that match exactly."

Step 5: Query. The user asks a question. That question gets embedded. ChromaDB finds the most similar chunks. Those chunks become the context. The LLM reads the context and generates a grounded answer.

Five steps. Load. Split. Embed. Store. Query. You will build this yourself in Lab 2.

You have built a RAG pipeline. It works in your notebook. But how do you know if it is actually good? And what breaks when you move to production?

Let us talk about evaluation first. There are two things to measure. Retrieval quality -- did you find the right chunks? And generation quality -- did the model use them correctly?

For retrieval, the question is simple: when someone asks a question, are the top chunks actually relevant? You can test this manually at first. Ask ten questions where you know the answer. Check if the right paragraphs show up. If they do not, your chunking or embedding strategy needs work.

For generation, the question is: did the model actually use the retrieved context, or did it ignore it and hallucinate anyway? This is more common than you think. Sometimes the model's training knowledge overrides the context you provide.

Common failures in production: chunks that are too small lose context. Chunks that are too big dilute the relevant information. Wrong chunks get retrieved because the question and the answer use different vocabulary.

Real-world use cases that work well with RAG: legal document analysis, customer support knowledge bases, technical documentation, internal policy search. Any domain where the answers live in your documents, not in the model's training data.

The Hallucination Problem

LLMs do not know what is true. They know what is likely. And that difference has real consequences.

What Is Hallucination?

Hallucination is when an AI model generates information that sounds confident and plausible but is factually incorrect. The model is not lying -- it is predicting the most likely next words, and sometimes those words form statements that are completely false.

Why Does It Happen?

LLMs are trained on massive datasets to predict the next token in a sequence. They learn patterns, not facts. When you ask a question outside their training data -- or within it but on a topic where many plausible answers exist -- the model generates what sounds right rather than what is right.

💥

The Problem

"Ask GPT about your company's Q4 revenue. It will fabricate numbers that look real -- complete with dollar signs, growth percentages, and quarter-over-quarter comparisons."

✅

The Solution

"Give the model your actual Q4 report as context. Now it quotes real numbers from your document -- grounded, verifiable, accurate."

The Cost of Hallucination

💰

Wrong Decisions

Fabricated data leads to bad business decisions based on numbers that do not exist.

🔒

Lost Trust

One hallucination destroys confidence in the entire system. Users stop trusting the tool.

⚖

Legal Risk

Fabricated legal citations, fake case law, and invented contract clauses create real liability.

The Fix: Retrieve, Then Generate

RAG -- Retrieval-Augmented Generation -- solves this by adding a retrieval step before generation. Instead of relying solely on what the model learned during training, RAG first searches your documents for relevant information, then passes that information to the model as context.

The model still generates the response, but now it has real data to work with. It is the difference between a closed-book exam and an open-book exam.

User asks question → Model guesses from training data → Confident but possibly wrong answer
✅
With RAG
User asks question → System retrieves relevant docs → Model generates answer grounded in real data

Embeddings & Vector Stores

Embeddings turn text into numbers that capture meaning. Vector stores make those numbers searchable. Together, they power semantic search.

What Are Embeddings?

An embedding is a list of numbers (a vector) that represents the meaning of a piece of text. The key insight: texts with similar meanings have similar vectors. "How do I return a product?" and "What is the refund policy?" have very different words but very similar embeddings -- because they mean nearly the same thing.

Similar words cluster together in embedding space. "King" is close to "Queen", far from "Bicycle."

What Are Vector Stores?

A vector store is a database optimized for similarity search. Instead of searching by exact keyword match, you search by meaning. You give it a query vector, and it returns the stored vectors that are most similar.

🚀

ChromaDB

Open-source, runs locally, great for prototyping. No server needed.

⚡

FAISS

Meta's library. Blazing fast for large datasets. More setup required.

🌱

Pinecone

Managed cloud service. Production-ready. Pay per usage.

Keyword Search vs Semantic Search

Feature Keyword Search Semantic Search
How it works Exact string matching Meaning-based similarity
Query: "refund policy" Only finds docs with "refund" or "policy" Also finds "how to return a product" and "money back guarantee"
Handles synonyms? No Yes
Best for Exact lookups, IDs, codes Natural language questions, Q&A

Chunking Strategies

How you split your documents determines the quality of your retrieval. The right chunking strategy depends on your data -- experiment.

A Document Split Into Chunks
Chunk 1: Introduction and background... Chunk 2: Methodology and approach... Chunk 3: Results and findings... Chunk 4: Conclusion and next steps...
Each chunk becomes its own embedding. Overlap between chunks preserves context at boundaries.

Strategy Comparison

Strategy How It Works Pros Cons
Fixed-Size Split every N characters Simple, predictable May cut mid-sentence
Paragraph-Based Split on paragraph breaks Preserves natural structure Uneven chunk sizes
Recursive Try paragraphs, then sentences, then words Smart, handles nested docs Slightly more complex
With Overlap Chunks share N characters at boundaries Context preserved across chunks More storage, slight redundancy

Recommended Approach

For most use cases, start with RecursiveCharacterTextSplitter with a chunk size of 500-1000 characters and an overlap of 50-100 characters. This gives you a good balance between context preservation and retrieval precision.

"The right chunking strategy depends on your documents. A legal contract needs different chunking than a product manual. Experiment."

Chunk Size: The Tradeoff

📏

Too Small

Loses context. The chunk might contain a sentence fragment that does not make sense on its own.

📖

Too Large

Dilutes relevance. The chunk contains so much text that the relevant part gets buried.
