Video 2: Tips for a Great Project

Prof on camera -- 4-5 minutes -- 5 segments
PART 01 Tip 1 -- Scope Ruthlessly
On Camera ~50 sec

Here is some advice from watching hundreds of student projects.

First, scope ruthlessly. The most common mistake is building too much. A polished, working demo of one thing beats a broken prototype of five things. Every time.

Shot Setup

Direct to camera, conversational. Count tips on fingers. This is mentor advice, not a lecture.

PART 02 Tip 2 -- Pick a Problem You Care About
On Camera ~40 sec

Second, pick a problem you care about. If you are in supply chain, build a supply chain tool. If you are in healthcare, build a healthcare tool. Your domain expertise combined with AI skills is what makes the project unique.

Shot Setup

Lean slightly forward. Personal, encouraging. This advice should feel like it comes from experience.

PART 03 Tip 3 -- Architecture Matters
On Camera ~40 sec

Third, architecture matters. Think about your system design before you write a single line of code. Which LLM? Which tools? How do components connect? A clean architecture is easier to build, easier to debug, and easier to present.

Storyboard -- Reference Architecture
PART 04 Tip 4 -- Demo Over Slides
On Camera ~30 sec

Fourth, demo over slides. When you present, show the working system. Click through it live. Nothing is more convincing than a system that works in front of the audience.

Shot Setup

Emphatic. This is the tip that separates good presentations from great ones.

PART 05 Tip 5 -- Document Everything
On Camera ~30 sec

Fifth, document everything. Your report should explain not just what you built, but why you made each technical decision. That is what separates an engineering project from a coding exercise.

Shot Setup

Measured delivery. "Engineering project from a coding exercise" is the key phrase -- let it land.

Video 3: Course Wrap-Up -- The Final Closing

Prof on camera -- 3 minutes -- 3 segments -- EMOTIONAL
PART 01 The Transformation
On Camera ~90 sec Medium close-up, warm lighting

You walked in as students learning about AI. You are walking out as engineers who build with it.

Over fourteen modules, you connected to AI through code. You built tools that write Excel formulas and analyze Bitcoin. You mastered prompt engineering. You forced AI output into structured, validated formats. You chained operations with LangChain. You grounded AI in real data with RAG. You built autonomous agents. You orchestrated multi-agent teams. You built frontends. You connected AI to every tool through MCP. You kept it safe with guardrails. You saw through AI's eyes with vision. You deployed with the latest SDKs. You automated workflows.

That is not a course. That is an engineering toolkit.

Shot Setup -- THE MOMENT

Just Prof. Dehghani on camera. Warm lighting. Direct to camera. No animations needed. No overlays. The words carry the moment. This is the emotional peak of the entire course. Shoot multiple takes -- find the one that feels real.

MEDIA TEAM: This is the most important 3 minutes of all 14 modules. Simple setup. Warm lighting, shallow depth of field. No graphics, no animations, no lower thirds. Just the professor and the camera. The enumeration of all 14 modules should build like a crescendo. Let it breathe.
PART 02 What Lasts
On Camera ~30 sec

The tools will change. New models will come. But the skill of knowing how to think about AI, how to architect with it, how to evaluate it. That does not expire.

Shot Setup

Slow, deliberate delivery. "That does not expire" is the thesis of the entire course. Hold the pause after.

PART 03 Go Build Something
On Camera ~15 sec

Thank you for being part of this course. Now go build something.

Final End Card
MEDIA TEAM: After Prof. says "Now go build something" -- hold on his face for 2 beats, then fade to end card. Music should be warm and resolving -- not triumphant, but satisfied. The end card should feel like closing a book.
