REVIEW -- First Draft

I was reviewing engineering drawings last month -- complex CAD diagrams with dozens of components. I uploaded one to GPT-4 Vision and asked it to identify potential stress points. It found three issues my team had missed. That is when I realized -- these models do not just read text anymore. They see.

For years, AI was a text-only game. You type, it responds. But something shifted. The latest models -- GPT-4V, Gemini, Claude -- they process images, audio, even video. They do not just read your question about a chart. They look at the chart.

This changes everything. Medical imaging. Manufacturing quality control. Document analysis. Architectural review. Any domain where visual information matters -- and that is most domains -- multimodal AI is now relevant.

But here is the catch. If these models can see, how do you know they are seeing correctly? How do you measure whether the output is good? That is the second half of this module -- evaluation. Because if you cannot measure it, you cannot trust it.

REVIEW -- First Draft

Let me show you how to send an image to an AI model. It is simpler than you think.

With GPT-4 Vision, you send a message that contains both text and an image. The image can be a URL or a base64-encoded file. The model processes both together and responds.

With Gemini Vision, the approach is similar but the API structure differs slightly. You can send images, PDFs, even short video clips.

What can these models actually do with images? Object detection, text extraction from photos, diagram analysis, comparing two images, describing scenes, reading handwriting. The accuracy varies by task, but for many practical applications it is remarkably good.

The key insight is this -- vision is not a separate capability. It is integrated into the same conversation. You can ask follow-up questions about an image. You can send multiple images and ask the model to compare them. It is a conversation, not a one-shot analysis.

REVIEW -- First Draft

You have built a RAG system. You have built agents. But here is the question nobody asks until it is too late -- how do you know if they are working well?

LLM evaluation is one of the hardest problems in AI engineering. Traditional software has unit tests -- input X, expect output Y. But LLMs are non-deterministic. The same input can produce different outputs every time.

So what do we measure? There are automated metrics like BLEU and ROUGE -- they compare generated text against reference text. Useful for translation and summarization. There is semantic similarity -- embedding the output and comparing it to expected answers. And there is human evaluation -- having people rate quality, accuracy, helpfulness.

For RAG systems specifically, you measure retrieval quality (did we find the right documents?) and generation quality (did the answer correctly use those documents?). Frameworks like RAGAS give you metrics for both.

The practical approach? Build a test suite. A set of questions with known good answers. Run your system against it regularly. Track the scores over time. That is regression testing for AI.

Multimodal AI

Multimodal AI refers to models that can process and reason across multiple types of input -- text, images, audio, and video -- within a single interaction.

What Does Multimodal Mean?

For most of AI history, models were unimodal. Text models processed text. Image models processed images. Speech models processed speech. Multimodal models break this boundary. You can send a photo of a whiteboard and ask the model to convert it to structured notes. You can send an audio clip and ask for a summary. You can mix all of these in a single conversation.

Which Models Support It?

GPT-4V / GPT-4o
Text + images. Accepts URLs and base64 images. Strong at diagram analysis, OCR, and visual Q&A.
Gemini 2.5
Text + images + audio + video. Native multimodal from the ground up. Handles PDFs and long video.
Claude 4
Text + images. Excellent at analyzing charts, documents, and screenshots with high accuracy.

Use Cases Across Industries

HealthcareMedical image analysis, radiology reportsImage + Text
ManufacturingVisual quality inspection, defect detectionImage + Text

RetailProduct identification, visual searchImage + Text

The Key Insight

Multimodal is not a feature -- it is the future of how we interact with AI. Text-only interfaces are a limitation of older technology, not a design choice. As models become natively multimodal, the distinction between "text AI" and "vision AI" will disappear entirely.

Why Evaluation Matters

You cannot deploy what you cannot measure. In traditional software, testing is straightforward -- given input X, expect output Y. With LLMs, the same input can produce different outputs every time. This makes evaluation both harder and more important.

The Evaluation Gap

Most teams building with LLMs skip evaluation entirely. They test manually -- "does the output look right?" -- and ship. This works until it does not. A prompt change that improves one use case silently breaks three others. A model update changes output quality in ways nobody notices for weeks.

Evaluation Frameworks

A proper evaluation framework has three components:

  • Test suite: A set of inputs with expected outputs or quality criteria
  • Metrics: Automated scores that measure output quality (BLEU, ROUGE, semantic similarity, custom rubrics)
  • Regression tracking: Running the test suite regularly and tracking scores over time

When to Evaluate

TriggerWhat to Check
Prompt changesRun full test suite, compare before/after
Model updatesRun full test suite with new model version
New data sources (RAG)Retrieval quality + generation quality
Production issuesAdd failing case to test suite, fix, verify

The teams that invest in evaluation early move faster in the long run. They can change prompts with confidence. They can upgrade models without fear. They can prove to stakeholders that their system works.

AI Startups in Vision

Vision AI is not theoretical. Real companies are building real products that process visual information at scale. Here are some of the most impactful.

Medical Imaging

PathAI -- AI-powered pathology. Analyzes tissue samples to detect cancer with higher accuracy than traditional methods. Used by pharma companies in drug development and clinical trials.

Autonomous Vehicles

Wayve -- End-to-end learned driving. Uses vision transformers to process camera feeds in real-time. Raised $1B+ for embodied AI that drives in complex urban environments.

Quality Control

Landing AI -- Founded by Andrew Ng. Visual inspection platform for manufacturing. Detects defects on production lines using computer vision models trained on small datasets.

Document Intelligence

Docugami -- AI document understanding. Processes complex business documents (contracts, invoices, reports) using vision and language models together.

What They Have in Common

Every successful vision AI company solves a specific, measurable problem. They do not sell "vision AI" -- they sell faster pathology results, fewer manufacturing defects, or automated document processing. The technology is the enabler, not the product.
