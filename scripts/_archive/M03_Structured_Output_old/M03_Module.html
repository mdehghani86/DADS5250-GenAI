Every morning I place the same coffee order. Medium dark roast, whole milk on the side, cinnamon on top. My barista knows it by heart. I never have to explain myself twice.

That is exactly the standard we should hold ourselves to when we communicate with AI models.

In Module 2, you built an Excel GPT and a Bitcoin analyzer. They worked -- mostly. But sometimes the output format was wrong. Sometimes the model gave you a paragraph when you needed a formula. Sometimes it went off track.

That inconsistency is not the model's fault. It is a prompting problem.

A well-written prompt is the difference between a useful tool and a random generator. And prompt engineering is the single most important skill in applied AI.

Let us start with what happens every time you talk to an AI model through code.

There are three layers. The system layer tells the model who it is -- its role, its rules, its constraints. The user layer is what you actually need -- your request. And the assistant layer is what was said before -- the conversation history.

Let me show you why this matters with one example.

I am going to ask the same question twice: "What should I do about my knee pain?" First, I set the system message to: "You are a compassionate family doctor." The model says: take rest, apply ice, consider physical therapy, and come see me if it persists.

Now I change the system message to: "You are a personal injury lawyer." Same question. The model says: document everything, get medical records, consider a second opinion, and if this happened at work, you may have a claim.

Same question. Completely different output. The only thing that changed was the system message. That is the power of the system layer.

Now let us talk about the four essential elements of a good prompt. Every prompt you write should have a Role, a Task, Context, and a Format.

Role: who is the model? Task: what do you need it to do? Context: what background information does it need? Format: what should the output look like?

Here is a bad prompt: "Tell me about renewable energy." That could mean anything. The model will give you a generic essay.

Here is the same request with all four elements: "You are an energy policy analyst. Summarize the top 3 renewable energy trends in 2026. Focus on cost reduction and grid integration. Output as a numbered list with one sentence per trend."

Role: energy policy analyst. Task: summarize trends. Context: cost and grid focus. Format: numbered list, one sentence each.

The difference is not subtle. It is the difference between useful output and noise.

You now know the structure. Let us talk about technique.

Zero-shot prompting means you give the model a task with no examples. "Classify this review as positive, negative, or neutral." It works surprisingly well for straightforward tasks. But watch what happens with edge cases.

"The food was fine, I guess." Is that positive, negative, or neutral? The model might say positive one time and neutral the next. That inconsistency is a problem when you need reliability.

Few-shot prompting fixes this. You give the model two or three examples before your actual request. "Great service!" -- Positive. "Terrible experience." -- Negative. "The food was okay." -- Neutral. Now classify: "The food was fine, I guess." The model consistently says Neutral. The examples lock in the format and the decision boundary.

Chain-of-thought is the most powerful technique. Let me show you with a famous trick question.

A bat and a ball cost $1.10 together. The bat costs $1.00 more than the ball. How much does the ball cost?

Most people -- and most AI models in zero-shot -- say 10 cents. That is wrong. If the ball is 10 cents, the bat is $1.10, and the total is $1.20.

Now add four words: "Think step by step." The model writes out the algebra. Let x equal the ball price. The bat is x plus one dollar. x plus x plus one equals 1.10. Two x equals 0.10. x equals 0.05. The ball costs 5 cents.

Four words. The correct answer. Wei et al. at Google Research showed in 2022 that chain-of-thought prompting improves accuracy on reasoning tasks by up to 40%.

Let me give you an engineering example. A factory produces 10,000 units per day. The defect rate is 2.3%. Each defect costs $47 to fix. What is the monthly defect cost assuming 22 working days?

Without chain-of-thought, the model sometimes gets this wrong -- it might forget to multiply by days, or round incorrectly. With "Think step by step," it lays out: 10,000 times 0.023 equals 230 defects per day. 230 times $47 equals $10,810 per day. $10,810 times 22 days equals $237,820 per month. Clean. Correct. Auditable.

System messages are the most underused tool in prompt engineering. Most people write "You are a helpful assistant" and move on. That is like giving an employee a job title and no job description.

Let me show you two real system messages that actually work.

Financial analyst: "You are a senior financial analyst. Provide data-driven analysis only. No hedging language -- no 'it depends' or 'it varies.' Every response must include specific numbers. Format: 3 paragraphs maximum, ending with one actionable recommendation."

Code reviewer: "You are a senior software engineer conducting code review. Focus on bugs and logic errors only -- not style or formatting. For every issue found, provide the fix as working code. End with a letter grade: A (no issues), B (minor issues), C (significant issues), F (critical bugs)."

These are not generic. They constrain the model. They define what success looks like. And they produce consistent, usable output.

Now let us talk about prompt templates. A template is a prompt with variables -- placeholders that change with each use.

Here is a template: "Analyze the following customer review for {product_name}. Identify the sentiment, key themes, and any actionable feedback. Output as JSON with keys: sentiment, themes, action_items."

Same template, three different inputs: a laptop review, a restaurant review, a software review. Three structured, consistent outputs. That is the power of templates.

And here is where it gets real. Imagine you have 10,000 customer reviews. One template. A Python loop. 20 minutes of processing time. About $3 in API costs. You just did what used to take an analyst a month.

That is prompt engineering at production scale.

This week you learned three levels of control. The system message controls who the model is. The prompt controls what it does. And the technique -- zero-shot, few-shot, chain-of-thought -- controls how well it does it.

You are no longer just sending messages to AI. You are engineering the communication. That is a fundamentally different skill.

Think back to Module 2. Your Excel GPT sometimes gave inconsistent output. Now you know why -- and you know how to fix it. Role. Task. Context. Format. Every time.

Next module -- what happens when you need the model to give you JSON, not prose? What if you need it to call a function? That is structured output and function calling. See you in Module 4.

The Anatomy of a Prompt

Every API call you have made follows a three-layer structure. Now you will master it.

The Three-Layer Framework

When you call an LLM API, every message belongs to one of three roles. Understanding these layers is the foundation of prompt engineering.

System Layer
Who the model is
Role, rules, constraints, personality. This message is set once and shapes every response that follows.
# Example
{"role": "system", "content": "You are a customer support agent for Acme Corp.
  Rules: Be concise. Never promise refunds. Always suggest the FAQ first."}
User Layer
What you need
The actual request -- a question, a task, data to analyze. This is where the 4 elements (Role, Task, Context, Format) live.
# Example
{"role": "user", "content": "My order #4821 arrived damaged. What are my options?"}
Assistant Layer
What was said before
Previous model responses in the conversation. This is how the model "remembers" context across turns.
# Example
{"role": "assistant", "content": "I am sorry about the damage. Please check our FAQ
  at acme.com/help for return instructions..."}

The 4 Essential Elements

Within the user message, the best prompts always contain these four components:

1. Role

Who is the model? "You are a senior data analyst."

2. Task

What do you need? "Summarize the top 3 trends."

3. Context

What background? "Focus on Q4 revenue data."

4. Format

What output shape? "Numbered list, one sentence each."

The Bad Prompts Museum

A gallery of terrible prompts and their terrible outputs. Learn from mistakes so you never make them.

"If your prompt could be about anything, the output will be about nothing."

Exhibit 1: The Vague Request

Bad Prompt
"Tell me about data"
Generic 500-word essay covering databases, big data, data science, and data privacy -- none in useful depth.
Fixed Prompt
"You are a data engineer. Explain the 3 most common data storage formats (CSV, Parquet, JSON) for a junior analyst. One paragraph each. Include when to use each."
Applied: Role + Task + Context + Format
Screenshot: ChatGPT response to "Tell me about data" vs. the structured version

Exhibit 2: The Context-Free Code Request

Bad Prompt
"Write code"
Random Python snippet -- a fibonacci function nobody asked for.
Fixed Prompt
"Write a Python function that reads a CSV file, calculates the average of the 'price' column, and returns the result. Use pandas. Include error handling for missing files."
Applied: Task (specific) + Context (pandas, CSV) + Format (function with error handling)

Exhibit 3: The Phantom Data

Bad Prompt
"Analyze this"
The model makes up data -- invents a dataset, generates fake statistics, and presents confident analysis of nothing.
Fixed Prompt
"Here is a list of 10 customer reviews: [paste reviews]. Classify each as Positive, Negative, or Neutral. Output as a table with columns: Review, Sentiment, Confidence."
Applied: Context (actual data provided) + Task (classify) + Format (table)

Exhibit 4: The Creative Chaos

Bad Prompt
"Be creative"
Unpredictable -- a poem one time, a story the next, a list of ideas the third. No consistency, no usability.
Fixed Prompt
"You are a marketing copywriter. Write 3 tagline options for a sustainable water bottle brand targeting college students. Each tagline should be under 8 words. Tone: playful but eco-conscious."
Applied: Role + Task + Context (audience) + Format (3 options, 8 words, tone)
The pattern is always the same: vague prompt = vague output.
Role. Task. Context. Format. Every time.

Building a Prompt Library

A prompt library is a collection of tested, reusable prompt templates. It is how teams move from ad-hoc prompting to systematic AI engineering.

Why Build a Prompt Library?

Consistency

Same prompt, same format, every time. No guessing.

Speed

New task? Start from a tested template, not a blank page.

Knowledge

Share what works across your team. Stop reinventing prompts.

Anatomy of a Library Entry

Each template in your library should include four things:

Role
Who the model should be for this task
Template
The prompt with {variable} placeholders
Examples
2-3 tested input/output pairs
Expected Format
What good output looks like (JSON, bullets, etc.)

4 Starter Templates

Template 1: Customer Feedback Analyzer
# System: You are a customer feedback analyst.
# User: Analyze this review for {product_name}:
"{review_text}"

Output JSON: {sentiment, key_themes[], action_items[], urgency: low/medium/high}
Template 2: Code Review Assistant
# System: You are a senior engineer. Focus on bugs, not style.
# User: Review this {language} code:
```{code}```

For each issue: describe bug, provide fix, rate severity (1-5).
End with overall grade: A/B/C/F.
Template 3: Meeting Notes Summarizer
# System: You are an executive assistant.
# User: Summarize these meeting notes from {meeting_date}:
"{notes}"

Output: 3 key decisions, action items with owners, next meeting date.
Template 4: Data Extraction from Documents
# System: You are a data extraction specialist. Be precise.
# User: Extract the following from this {document_type}:
"{document_text}"

Return JSON: {name, date, total_amount, line_items[], payment_method}

Key Takeaways

  • Three-layer framework: System messages control who the model is, user messages control what it does, assistant messages provide conversation history.

  • System messages are job descriptions: Specific constraints, behavioral rules, and output format requirements produce reliable, production-grade output.
  • Prompt templates scale: One tested template + a Python loop = 10,000 consistent analyses. That is the bridge from experiment to product.
